THE BIG IDEA

A HIGHER ED. LABORATORY

About five years ago we started thinking about some big problems. We saw students lives stifled by the $1 Trillion in college debt, the lack of connection to communities, and the disconnect between theory and practice. After four years of volunteer development, the Saxifrage School is now up and running. We talk about this current stage of our work as a "higher education laboratory". We come up with new ideas on how to do college better and test them out. Over the last year we have chosen a campus neighborhood, held over a dozen classes, launched the Pittsburgh Forum, worked on student-voice research with the Sprout Fund, developed the Askr concept, and launched Dev.Year. It's exciting to think that we have grown enough that, if a student wanted, they could study with us full-time. The current cost of a full course load (four twelve-week classes) would be about $1800 per semester. Although we are not a "college" and do not offer credit, we do offer learners the resources and community they need to begin their life's work. By lowering costs and re-thinking the campus we are able to focus on preparing students to make what is valuable and to question the value of what is made. Throughout its history our effort has been supported by hundreds of volunteers and thoughtful students. Considering joining our work by donating, writing us, or taking an upcoming course. Join us in making sure, as Thoreau says in Walden, that we "do not play life, or study it merely, while the community supports [us] at this expensive game, but earnestly live it from beginning to end."

THINGS YOU CAN LEARN WITH US

THE COURSES

Instead of a schedule of classes, we'll put you into a community of developers, give you a fieldbook, and help you build projects.

THE COMMUNITY

JOIN

Join a team and start learning together. Join the broader community by getting active on GitHub, mailing lists, twitter, and IRC. Attend Meetups, user groups, and conferences with your team. We'll have over 50 developers join us throughout the year, and you'll get to help set the weekly schedules.

MENTOR

Be mentored and mentor others. Your team will meet weekly with a mentor to set goals and celebrate accomplishments. They will work closely with you, providing resources and inspiration. They're learning too, but will be there with advice and help when you need it.

FEAST

We'll hold two weekly meals and invite guest experts in to talk about their favorite subjects. Talk SASS over stew and privacy over pancakes. We'll discuss the schedule of a third weekly meal, allowing you to customize the year based on what your team needs.

BUILD

Start by working on small projects to serve needs in the community. Take on increasingly challenging work with the help of experienced collaborators. Over the year, build a portfolio of your work and start to contribute to open-source projects.

WORKSHOP

Tinker and learn new skills. Expert guests will join us multiple times per week to help you dive into new languages and overcome tricky learning challenges. Workshops won't just be on code. We can get into desk-building, logic, poetry, and occupational health as well.

DISCUSS

Exchange ideas and start to ask hard questions about your work. Read important texts on the past, present and future of the internet, on hacker culture, and autodidacticism. Discuss critical questions concerning the future of the open web.

The schedule for the year is, to some degree, up to you and what you want to build. We'll provide the best community and resources we can, but we've realized that if we plan your year for you, you won't be working towards what's next. As Calvin says to Miss Wormwood, "You can present the material, but you can't make me care." It's kind of like getting a bulletin in a church service or reading the translation at the Opera. You become a passive audience member with your attention on the content, not your experience. The success of your year requires serious engagement. We want you to help you start your life's work now. Of course, there is something valuable about being able to answer the question "What should I learn next?" We won't give you an entire schedule for the year, but we'll give you a Fieldbook on day one. Instead of setting your year in stone, this book will give you some good ideas on first projects, what to learn next, some texts to read, and highlight some core principles. Think of it as a really awesome "Hacker Scout" handbook. We're going to spend a lot of time crafting this Dev.Year Fieldbook before you join us. Not only will it be packed full of great resources, it will provide a basic structure for the Dev.Year community. The Fieldbook will help you set goals for your work and visualize your progress. We're not going to give you any badges for your sash. Sorry. We do hope the Fieldbook finds a permanent spot in your knapsack and gives you an inspiring picture of what's possible with code. Below is a roughly drafted outline of the fieldbook: a three-part guide for starting and an overview of its resources. It's a brief picture of everything you could learn throughout the year. The Fieldbook is, in no small part, inspired by the Boy Scouts of America, ESR's "How to Become a Hacker" and why's "(poignant) guide to ruby".

THE FIELDBOOK


I. STARTING

Learn what things are and how they work: computer, os, program, server, client, language, internet, url. Build a personal site from scratch and setup your workspace: get git and software, build a desk, etc. Challenge yourself to find the best help and to fall in love with code. Read hacker FAQs.

II. GOOD THINGS TO DO NEXT

Learn to use Terminal, publish your personal blog using Git, dig into HTML/CSS, and one dynamic, object-oriented language. Start building a web platform using what you've learned. Challenge yourself to use design methods in your work and study UX. Start re-writing parts of this fieldbook.


III. KEEP GOING

Learn C and get to know UNIX. Work with another object-oriented language, multiple web frameworks, and JS/JQuery. Help the community build an open-source project by becoming a consistent contributor. Start your own project with a team. Challenge yourself to write weekly and fall in love with a problem you want to solve.


IV. CODE

A guide of the best resources for learning everything you need.

Git, Terminal, Systems/Servers, C, Ruby, Python, PHP, web frameworks, JS/JQuery, CSS/SASS/LESS, HTML, Databases, APIs, Testing, text editors, etc.


V. BUILD

A guide full of little projects to learn on and ideas for what to build next.

"Hello World", yourself.com, "Terminal for a Week", Open-Source projects to start on, Arduino, Raspberry Pi, build a web server, etc.


VI. WONDER

A guide and compendium of texts that inform the way we work and why.

Logic, poetry, mathematics, history of computation, history of the web, hacker culture, open source theory, privacy, security, economics, culture, comic books, games, education, etc.

OUR HOPES FOR THE YEAR'S END

GOALS

1. OPEN-SOURCE

By the end of the year, we hope every student will be a contributing member of an open-source project.

2. PORTFOLIO

By the end of the year, we want you to be certified by the community you serve. Your GitHub profile, personal portfolio website, twitter history, and relationships will be a record of your work.

3. FULL-STACK

A year isn't long enough to master anything, but we really want you to learn some of everything. Working with the full stack with help you see the scope of what you can do and how everything connects: databases, terminal, git, CSS, HTML, JQuery, design methods, testing, APIs, etc.

4. DON'T STOP

Once the year is over, keep going. While we won't give you the same dedicated attention, you're still part of the community. Stay with us as a mentor, attend workshops, come to meals, and contribute to open source projects. Keep coding with your team and start a company.

5. BE A VOICE

In order to find your voice you'll have to spend a lot of time reading and listening. Like most technology, the internet has great potential to create and destroy. Be a creative voice and start contributing to the culture and literature of the web.

6. BE NATIVE

The technology and employment opportunities make it easy to be a place-less webworker that works on big universal projects. We think there is serious value is getting to know a place really well. Become native to Pittsburgh and work with other natives to address real local issues.
